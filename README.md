# LaminarPipeflow

A verification study of incompressible, fully developed laminar flow in a circular pipe.  

The simulation performed in __Ansys Fluent__ is compared to the analytical Hagen–Poiseuille solution for velocity profile and pressure drop.

## 1. Problem Definition

The length of the pipe, L, is chosen such as to ensure fully developed flow at the outlet

**Flow Regime**  
The Reynolds number is defined as:

$$Re = \frac{\rho U D}{\mu}$$

, where 
- $D$ is the diameter of the pipe, 
- $\rho$ it the fluid density,
- $U$ is the __mean flow velocity__,
- $\mu$ is the __dynamic viscosity__.

The inlet velocity and fluid properties are chosen such that the Reynolds number as given by the equation above, is below 2300. Therefore, the flow remains strictly _laminar_

**Analytical Solution**   

The velocity profile is given by:

$$u(r) = U_{\max}\left(1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^2\right)\qquad (1)$$

The pressure drop as given by the Hagen–Poiseuille equation is:

$$\Delta p = \frac{32 \mu U L}{D^2}\qquad(2)$$

, where L is the length of the pipe.

Expression (1) and (2) serves as the reference for validation.

## 2. Mesh Strategy

BILLEDE MESH

**Overall approach**  
A structured, body‑fitted mesh is used to resolve the circular cross‑section and the near‑wall gradients accurately. The mesh is generated as a 2D axisymmetric or 3D pipe, depending on the solver setup, but the meshing principles are the same.

- **Topology:** Structured (hexahedral) where possible, or high‑quality quad/prism layers near the wall
- **Alignment:** Cells aligned with the axial flow direction to minimize numerical diffusion
- **Symmetry:** Exploits geometric symmetry to keep the mesh efficient and clean

This topology is well suited for laminar, fully developed pipe flow and for direct comparison with the analytical solution.

###  Near‑wall resolution

**Boundary layer treatment**  
Even though the flow is laminar, accurate prediction of the parabolic velocity profile and wall shear stress requires sufficient resolution close to the wall.

- **Radial grading:** Cells are gradually refined towards the wall using a growth ratio < 1.2
- **First cell height:** Chosen such that the non‑dimensional wall distance $y^+$ remains $\ll 1$ for the given Reynolds number

This ensures that the viscous sublayer is fully resolved and that the wall shear stress—and thus the pressure drop—can be compared reliably to the Hagen–Poiseuille prediction.

### 2.3 Axial resolution

**Streamwise discretization**  
The mesh is refined along the pipe axis to capture any developing region near the inlet and to ensure a smooth, fully developed profile at the outlet.

- **Uniform or mildly graded cells** in the axial direction
- **Sufficient number of cells per diameter** to avoid excessive aspect ratios
- **Refinement in the validation region** where velocity profiles and pressure drop are extracted

This provides a clean numerical environment for comparing the CFD solution to the analytical profile.

---

### 2.4 Mesh quality metrics

**Quality checks**  
Standard mesh quality metrics are monitored to ensure solver robustness and accuracy:

- **Orthogonality:** High orthogonality to reduce discretization error
- **Skewness:** Kept well below typical solver warning thresholds
- **Aspect ratio:** Controlled, especially near the wall, to balance accuracy and stability

Only meshes that satisfy these quality criteria are used for the final simulations.

---

### 2.5 Mesh independence study

**Verification of mesh adequacy**  
To demonstrate that the numerical solution is not sensitive to the chosen mesh, a mesh independence study is performed:

- **Coarse, medium, and fine meshes** are generated by systematically refining both radial and axial resolutions
- **Key metrics compared:**
    - Centerline and radial velocity profiles
    - Pressure drop over the pipe length
- **Convergence criterion:** Changes in these quantities between successive refinements fall below a predefined tolerance

This step shows that the final mesh is not just “good looking” but **quantitatively sufficient** for validating the laminar pipe‑flow solution.
# 3. Boundary Conditions

**Inlet**

- Velocity inlet
- Uniform axial velocity $U$

**Outlet**

- Pressure outlet 0 gauge pressure
- Fully developed flow expected

**Wall**

- No‑slip condition

**Axis (2D axisymmetric)**

- Axis boundary condition

# 4. Solver Setup

**Flow Model**

Assumption: 

- Steady‑state
- Incompressible
- Laminar model

**Numerics**

- Pressure–velocity coupling: SIMPLE / SIMPLEC
- Momentum: second‑order upwind or central
- Pressure: second‑order

**Convergence Criteria**

- Residuals < $10^{-6}$
- Mass imbalance $< 0.1\%$
- Linear pressure drop along pipe
